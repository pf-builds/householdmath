How this is calculated

The math is straightforward, the judgment is in the inputs.

FigureHow it's found
Fully funded targetMonthly essential expenses × target months of coverage
Months covered nowCurrent savings ÷ monthly essential expenses
Gap to closeTarget − current savings (never below zero)
Time to fully fundedGap ÷ monthly amount saved, rounded up to whole months

The time estimate assumes a steady monthly contribution and does not add interest earned on the balance, which for a short-term, safety-first fund is small enough to leave out of a planning estimate.

Where the 3-to-6-month guideline comes from

Three to six months of essential expenses is the range most commonly cited in consumer financial education, including materials from the U.S. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) and the FDIC's Money Smart program. It is guidance, not a rule with legal force. A dual-income household with stable jobs may be comfortable at the lower end, a single earner, a commission-based income, or a field with long job searches points toward the higher end or beyond, which is why 9 months and a custom figure are offered.

Start small if the full target feels out of reach

The CFPB and most planners suggest building a small starter fund first, often a few hundred to a couple thousand dollars, so an unexpected bill doesn't go on a credit card, then growing it toward the full target over time. A partial fund is far better than none.

What this doesn't account for

  • Interest or yield earned on the balance (deliberately excluded, it's minor over a short horizon).
  • Irregular income, average it into a monthly figure first.
  • Whether to prioritize the fund over paying down high-interest debt, that's a personal trade-off, not a calculation.

Common questions

How many months of expenses should an emergency fund cover?

Three to six months of essentials is the common guideline. Single or variable incomes often aim for six to nine or more. It depends on how stable your income is and how fast you could replace it.

What counts as an essential expense?

Anything you'd still owe with no income: housing, utilities, food, transportation, insurance, and minimum debt payments. Discretionary spending is left out because you'd cut it in a real emergency.

Emergency fund or pay off debt first?

Common guidance: a small starter fund first, then high-interest debt, then finish the full fund. The right order for you is a personal decision this tool doesn't make.
