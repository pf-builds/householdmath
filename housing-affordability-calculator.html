How this is calculated

The estimate applies the 28/36 rule, a long-standing conventional-lending guideline, and uses whichever of the two limits is stricter for you.

LimitWhat it means
Front-end, 28%Total monthly housing payment (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, HOA) stays at or below 28% of gross monthly income.
Back-end, 36%All monthly debt, housing plus car, student, and card payments, stays at or below 36% of gross monthly income.

The calculator takes the smaller of the two resulting housing payments, subtracts your estimated taxes, insurance, and HOA to isolate principal and interest, then works backward through the standard mortgage-payment formula at your rate and term to find the loan you can support. Adding your down payment gives the price. The 28/36 thresholds are widely published in consumer lending education, including CFPB materials; some loan programs allow higher back-end ratios (often up to 43% or more) with strong credit or reserves, which is why real approvals can differ from this guide.

Gross income, not take-home

Lenders measure these ratios against gross, pre-tax income, so the estimate does too. Your monthly comfort is better measured against take-home pay, a payment that qualifies on paper can still strain an actual budget. It's worth checking the resulting payment against the budget calculator on a take-home basis.

What this doesn't account for

  • Credit score, employment history, and cash reserves, all of which affect approval and rate.
  • Private mortgage insurance (PMI), typically required with less than 20% down.
  • Local property-tax and insurance rates, entered here as an estimate to confirm for your area.
  • Closing costs and maintenance, ongoing costs of ownership beyond the loan.

Common questions

What is the 28/36 rule?

Housing payment at or below 28% of gross monthly income, total debt at or below 36%. A common conventional guideline; the calculator uses whichever is stricter for you. Lenders vary and some allow more.

Why gross income, not take-home?

Underwriting measures debt-to-income on gross pay, so the estimate matches. A qualifying payment can still feel tight against take-home, check it in the budget calculator too.

What does this leave out?

Credit, reserves, loan program, PMI under 20% down, and local tax and insurance rates. It's a planning figure, not a pre-qualification.
