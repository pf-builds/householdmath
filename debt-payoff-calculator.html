      How this is calculated

      Both methods run the same monthly simulation. The only difference is which debt gets your extra payment first.

      StepWhat happens each month
      1. InterestEach balance grows by its APR ÷ 12.
      2. MinimumsEvery debt gets its minimum payment.
      3. ExtraLeftover budget goes to the target debt, highest APR (avalanche) or smallest balance (snowball).
      4. RolloverWhen a debt is cleared, its freed-up payment joins the extra and rolls to the next target. Your total monthly payment stays constant.

      Why avalanche costs less, and when snowball still wins

      Directing every extra dollar at the highest interest rate first is, mathematically, the order that pays the least total interest, that's the avalanche, and it's not a matter of opinion. The snowball targets the smallest balance instead, so a debt disappears from the list sooner. Published research on consumer debt (Gal and McShane, "Can Small Victories Help Win the War? Evidence from Consumer Debt Management," Journal of Marketing Research, 2012) found that clearing small balances early is associated with a higher chance of eliminating all debt, momentum matters. If the interest gap between the two is small, the method you'll actually stick with is the right one.

      What this assumes

      • Fixed APRs and fixed minimum payments (real minimums usually shrink as balances fall, which slightly slows real-world payoff).
      • Interest compounding monthly at APR ÷ 12, applied before payments.
      • No new charges or borrowing on any of the debts.
      • Your total monthly payment stays the same until you're debt-free.

      Where a minimum payment is smaller than a month's interest, that balance grows until the strategy targets it. If nothing is ever paid off, raising the extra payment is the lever.

      Common questions

      What's the difference between avalanche and snowball?

      Both pay minimums everywhere and aim extra at one debt, then roll it onward. Avalanche targets the highest interest rate (least interest paid); snowball targets the smallest balance (fastest individual wins).

      Does avalanche always cost less?

      In total interest, yes. But snowball's early wins are linked to better follow-through in research, so the cheaper-on-paper method isn't automatically the better one for you.

      What does this assume?

      Fixed rates and minimums, monthly compounding, no new borrowing, and a constant total monthly payment. It's a planning estimate, not advice.
