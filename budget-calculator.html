How this is calculated

Your entries are grouped into three buckets and each is measured as a share of your take-home income, then compared to the 50/30/20 guideline.

BucketWhat's in itBenchmark
NeedsHousing, transportation, food≈ 50% of take-home
WantsDiscretionary spending≈ 30% of take-home
Savings & debtSavings, investing, debt payments≈ 20% of take-home

A bucket is flagged as running hot when it exceeds its benchmark share. The summary compares everything you allocated against your take-home pay: a positive figure is money not yet assigned, a negative figure means your planned spending is larger than your income.

Where the 50/30/20 rule comes from

The 50/30/20 framework was popularized in the 2005 book All Your Worth: The Ultimate Lifetime Money Plan by Elizabeth Warren and Amelia Warren Tyagi. The U.S. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) publishes budgeting worksheets built on the same needs/wants/savings structure. It is a widely used starting point, not a rule with legal force, and it fits some situations poorly, high housing-cost areas routinely push needs above 50%, which is why the benchmark is shown as a guide rather than a pass/fail line.

A note on where debt payments go

This tool counts debt payments in the 20% savings-and-debt bucket, the most common modern reading of the rule. Some versions instead treat minimum payments as a need and count only extra payoff toward the 20%. The grouping changes the labels, not the underlying question: whether your essential costs leave enough room to save and pay down what you owe.

Handling annual and irregular costs

In Build mode, set any line's frequency to quarterly or annual and the calculator converts it to a monthly figure for you, so a $1,200 yearly insurance bill counts as $100 a month. In Check mode, the three bucket fields are monthly, and the separate "Annual & irregular costs" field covers yearly bills the same way. Either way you no longer have to do that division by hand.

What this doesn't account for

  • Income that varies month to month, or one-time windfalls.
  • The interest cost or payoff order of your specific debts, that's what the debt payoff calculator is for.

Common questions

What is the 50/30/20 budgeting rule?

Aim for roughly 50% of take-home pay on needs, 30% on wants, and 20% on savings and extra debt repayment. It's a rule of thumb popularized in the 2005 book All Your Worth, useful as a starting point rather than a fixed target.

Which category do my debt payments go in?

Here they sit with savings in the 20% bucket. Some versions count minimum payments as a need instead. The total picture matters more than the exact bucket.

How do I handle annual or irregular costs?

In Build mode, set a line's frequency to quarterly or annual and it's converted to a monthly figure automatically. In Check mode, use the "Annual & irregular costs" field. It's a planning estimate, not financial advice.
